QUANTUM COMPUTERS

What are quantum computers?

Quantum computers are information processing devices that use the laws of quantum mechanics, especially superposition, entanglement and interference to represent and manipulate data in a fundamentally different way from classical computers.

Fundamentals of Quantum Mechanics (1900-1930)

Superposition

A quantum system can exist simultaneously in multiple possible states until measured. For example, an electron can be in different positions or spins at the same time, and only "chooses" a defined state when observed.

Entanglement

Quantum particles can correlate in such a way that the state of one depends instantaneously on the state of the other, even when separated by large distances. This creates a connection that doesn't exist in classical physics.

Click to learn more

Interference

Like waves, quantum states can overlap and combine, reinforcing or canceling probabilities of certain outcomes. A classic example is the double-slit experiment, where particles show interference patterns like waves.

Superposition

Erwin Schrödinger: Created the Schrödinger equation and the Schrödinger's Cat experiment, illustrating that quantum systems can exist in multiple states simultaneously.

Werner Heisenberg: Created matrix mechanics and the Uncertainty Principle, reinforcing the idea of quantum states in superposition.

×

Entanglement

Albert Einstein, Boris Podolsky and Nathan Rosen (EPR): Proposed the EPR paradox, showing that particles can be correlated at a distance ("spooky action at a distance").

Later John Bell created Bell's Theorems, allowing experimental testing of entanglement.

×

Interference

Thomas Young: Demonstrated wave interference with light in the double-slit experiment.

Niels Bohr: Interpreted interference in terms of quantum probabilities (Copenhagen interpretation).

Paul Dirac: Formalized the wave function and explained interference as a result of superposition of quantum states.

First Ideas of Quantum Computing
1959 – Richard Feynman

Suggests that quantum phenomena can only be simulated with quantum systems

1970s – Paul Benioff

Formulates a theoretical model of a reversible quantum computer

1981 – MIT Conference

Feynman presents the idea of a universal quantum computer

Theoretical Advances and Fundamental Algorithms
1985 – David Deutsch proposes the first universal quantum computer model

Deutsch introduced the concept of universal quantum logic gate, showing that quantum computers could perform any classical computational operation and more, formalizing the theoretical basis of modern quantum computing.

1994 – Peter Shor creates Shor's Algorithm

Shor developed an algorithm capable of factoring large numbers exponentially faster than classical methods. This advance showed that quantum computers could break RSA encryption, demonstrating their revolutionary potential in digital security.

Lov Grover develops Grover's Algorithm

Grover proposed an algorithm to search information in unordered databases with quadratic complexity lower than classical. Despite being simple, the algorithm demonstrated how quantum algorithms can significantly accelerate specific tasks.

First Experiments

University of Massachusetts (1998)

2-qubit NMR quantum computer, running the Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm.

University of Massachusetts researchers implemented 2 NMR qubits in chloroform molecules, manipulating nuclear spins to run the complete Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm. The experiment confirmed that physical qubits can be controlled to solve computational problems, consolidating the practical feasibility of quantum computing.

IBM & Stanford University (1998)

First experimental quantum computer with 2 qubits using NMR.

IBM and Stanford researchers created a 2-qubit quantum computer using NMR in liquid molecules. They manipulated nuclear spins with radio pulses to perform quantum logic gates, demonstrating superposition and interference. They executed a simplified version of the Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm, showing quantum advantage over classical methods.

2001 – IBM - First "truly executed" algorithm

The IBM 2001 was a milestone in quantum computing, consisting of a 7-qubit computer using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). It successfully executed Shor's algorithm, capable of factoring integers faster than classical computers, demonstrating in practice fundamental concepts of quantum computing, such as superposition and interference.

Although small and limited to a controlled environment, it was the first experiment to run a complete quantum algorithm, proving that complex quantum calculations were possible and opening the way for larger and commercial prototypes.

D-Wave One - 2011

The D-Wave One, launched in 2011 by Canadian company D-Wave Systems, was the world's first commercial quantum computer. Using 128 superconducting qubits and operating at cryogenic temperatures, it was designed to solve optimization problems and research complex solutions more efficiently than classical computers.

Its development began with 16 and 28-qubit prototypes, evolving to the stable 128-qubit model. The first unit was installed at Lockheed Martin's Technology Institute, and the system required isolation against vibrations, radiation and magnetic fields.

Companies and institutions such as NASA and Google also acquired the D-Wave One for research and practical applications, marking the transition of quantum computing from experimental to commercial environment.

D-Wave One Quantum Processor

The D-Wave One quantum processor, called Rainier, has 128 superconducting qubits.

The chip itself is only a few millimeters, but the complete equipment occupies almost the size of a wardrobe.

Current Quantum Computers

IBM Condor

Qubits: 1,121

Release: 2023

Released in 2023, the IBM Condor is the first quantum processor with over 1,000 qubits, marking a major step in quantum scalability. It is accessible via cloud and integrates the IBM Quantum System Two, combining classical and quantum computing.

Google Willow

Qubits: 105

Release: 2024

Google Willow, successor to Sycamore, has 105 qubits and was developed focusing on error correction and quantum stability. It is part of Google's strategy to achieve fault-tolerant quantum computing.

D-Wave Advantage2

Qubits: >5,000

Release: 2024

The D-Wave Advantage2 is D-Wave's latest generation system, with over 5,000 qubits. Designed to solve large-scale optimization problems, it is used in logistics, energy and finance.
